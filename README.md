# Bari rental investment verdict for €100,000

**Bari is a qualified "yes" for a €100,000 EU investor — but only with the right strategy and the right neighborhood.** The optimal play is a 3-bedroom student rental in San Pasquale Bassa, Carrassi, or Picone, targeting a realistic **8–9% net yield** at the *canone concordato studente* 10% flat tax. Short-term tourist rentals offer higher gross returns but face tightening regulation, 44% median occupancy, and a saturated supply (2,412 active listings); long-term *libero* contracts expose the absentee landlord to Italy's 12–24-month eviction risk. Bari sits at a structural sweet spot — Puglia's most dynamic city, port and airport at record passenger levels, the Naples–Bari high-speed rail arriving 2027–2028, and 50,000+ university students competing for a shrinking long-term rental supply — but the budget is tight for the prime tourist zones (Murat, Bari Vecchia, Madonnella core), forcing a deliberate trade-off between location prestige and rental yield. The sections below quantify each lever and lay out a concrete action plan.

## The Bari market in numbers

The Bari residential market is the **most expensive in southern Italy after Naples**, and rents are growing faster than almost anywhere else in the country. As of March 2026, Immobiliare.it puts the city average at **€2,255/sqm sale and €13.01/sqm/month rent**, with Idealista corroborating at €2,059/sqm and €13.10/sqm. Sale prices rose roughly +4% in 2024 and **+6.7% city-wide in H1 2025** (Tecnocasa), with the Centro macro-zone up +12.1% in the same period. Rent growth has been even hotter: **+10.3% YoY in 2025** (second-fastest in Italy after Genova), and Madonnella alone posted a remarkable +22.7% in Q3 2025 — a signal of supply tightness as long-term stock migrates to short-let platforms. Forecasts for 2026 cluster at **+4–5.5% on sale, +5.5–9.3% on rent** (Immobiliare.it Insights).

Compared to the Italian peer set, Bari trades at a premium to all other Puglia cities (**Lecce €1,624/sqm, Brindisi €1,116/sqm, Taranto €951/sqm, Foggia €1,128/sqm**) but a discount to Naples (€2,915/sqm) and roughly in line with the 12-city national average of €2,139/sqm. Implied **city-wide gross yield is ~6.9–7.1%**, materially better than Milan or Rome. Foreign investment in Puglia hit record levels in 2025: Scenari Immobiliari reports €5.5bn of foreign-family Italian home purchases nationally, with **Puglia capturing the largest share of any region** (2,300 of 8,600 transactions 2020–2025). Most foreign capital, however, lands in Valle d'Itria (Ostuni, Polignano, Monopoli) at €3,500–6,000/sqm — Bari city remains a domestic-investor and student/professional rental market.

## The three rental strategies, head-to-head

The strategy decision matters more than the neighborhood decision. Each path produces a fundamentally different risk-return-workload profile.

### Short-term tourist rentals — high gross, complicated reality

AirROI's February 2026 Bari report is the cleanest dataset: **2,412 active listings, median ADR $131 (~€120), median annual revenue $16,074 (~€14,700), median occupancy 44.1%**, with YoY revenue growth of +24.3%. Performance dispersion is enormous — top-decile listings exceed €30,000/year, but the bottom quartile struggles below €8,000. **Seasonality is severe**: peak August earns ~$2,855/month at 60% occupancy; January–February crashes to ~$842/month at 23%. After 30–35% operating costs (cleaning, platform fees, utilities, condo, IMU, mandatory RC insurance) and 21% cedolare secca, **net yield typically lands at 6–11%**, with top performers achieving more. Demand drivers are objectively strong: **935,135 tourists and 2.09M overnight stays in Bari in 2024**, with 2025 already on track to exceed those levels by ~20%; **550,817 cruise passengers in 2025** (+19% YoY) with a new MSC terminal opening summer 2026.

The catch is **regulatory load**. Since 1 January 2025, every unit needs a **CIN (Codice Identificativo Nazionale)** plus a Puglia **CIS** code, SCIA/CIA filing, mandatory civil liability insurance (Puglia *Determinazione 217/2025* effective October 2025), CO and gas detectors, certified UNI 9994-1 fire extinguishers, DM 37/2008 electrical compliance, PayTourist tourist-tax collection (€1.50/night in Bari for STR), Alloggiati Web 24-hour police reporting, and ISTAT statistical filings. The **2026 budget law lowered the entrepreneurial threshold** from 5 to 3 properties — meaning from the 3rd unit you trigger mandatory VAT registration and lose cedolare secca. For a single-property investor this is manageable; for a portfolio it is a hard ceiling.

### Long-term residential — stable income, high tenant risk

A central 1-bed at €13.5/sqm × 45 sqm yields **~€600/month or €7,200/year gross**; a periphery 2-bed at €9/sqm × 75 sqm hits **~€675/month or €8,100/year**. Net yields land at **5–6.5%** after costs and 21% *cedolare secca libero* (or 10% if using *canone concordato*). Demand is robust thanks to Bari's role as Puglia's regional service hub — Policlinico hospital staff, court workers, public-sector employees, transferring professionals — and **tenant search times of 2–6 weeks** in well-located zones.

The killer risk is **eviction**. Italian tenant protection is among Europe's strongest: a *sfratto per morosità* legally starts after 20 days of non-payment but takes **6 months to 2+ years** in practice; tenants can request a 90-day grace period and get 6–18 month suspensions if unemployed, over 65, or supporting 5+ children. Eight million Italian properties sit vacant partly due to this risk. For an absentee EU landlord, a 4+4 *libero* contract is the wrong instrument. The right instruments are **transitorio (1–18 months)** for transferring professionals or **canone concordato studente (6–36 months)**, both of which terminate cleanly and qualify for the 10% flat tax.

### Student rental — the optimal strategy for this profile

Bari hosts one of Italy's largest student populations: **Università di Bari "Aldo Moro" with ~50,000–60,000 students** (≈40,000 in Bari proper) and **Politecnico di Bari with ~11,000**, plus LUM and others. Of the combined ~50,000+ Bari-resident students, an estimated **20,000–25,000 are *fuori-sede*** seeking private accommodation, against a public ADISU dorm capacity of only a few thousand beds. RE/MAX Italia explicitly reports that "traditional long-term contracts have almost disappeared" in Bari as units flow to short-let — meaning **chronic excess demand from students**.

Per-room rents (April 2026 data from Idealista, Bakeca, RentYourNest): **central singola €400–€450, mid-zone Carrassi/Picone/San Pasquale €310–€350, peripheral €250**. Renting a typical 70–90 sqm 3-bedroom in San Pasquale, Carrassi, or Picone room-by-room yields **€930–€1,160/month** = **€11,000–€14,000/year gross**. Under *canone concordato studente* the **10% cedolare secca** — half the *libero* rate — boosts net yields meaningfully. Effective occupancy is 90–95% with September–August contracting. The result: **net yields of 7–9.5%**, structurally competitive with median STR returns *without* the regulatory load, summer crash, or 50%+ operating costs.

## The full cost stack — what €100K really commits

Acquisition costs add **12–17% on top of the purchase price**, most likely +13–15%. For a €100K resale from a private seller: registration tax 9% on the *cadastral value* (typically €4,000–€5,000 in cash, since cadastral values run 30–60% below market), fixed cadastral and mortgage taxes €100, notary €2,300–€3,500 including spese, agency 3% + VAT (~€3,660), interpreter €600–€1,800, independent legal/geometra due diligence €1,200–€2,500, and €500–€1,000 for codice fiscale, surveyor, miscellaneous. **Total: €12,360–€17,560 on top of price.**

Ongoing annual costs run **€2,200–€4,500** before management fees: IMU at Bari's 1.06% second-home rate yields **€700–€1,200** for a typical small apartment (the cadastral base, not market value, is the multiplier); TARI €200–€350; *spese condominiali* €400–€1,500 depending on lift and services; insurance including the now-mandatory RC liability €200–€500; maintenance reserve €1,000+; utilities standing charges €600–€900 if not tenant-paid. **Property management eats further**: full-service STR managers in Bari (CleanBnB, Italianway, Halldis, Apulia Hospitality, local co-hosts) charge **18–25% of gross revenue + VAT**; long-term management runs 8–10% of annual rent + VAT.

Tax on rental income is favorable by EU standards: **10% cedolare secca** for *canone concordato* (student or 3+2), 21% for *libero* or first STR property, 26% for STR from the second property onward. Capital gains: **26% if sold within 5 years, fully exempt thereafter** under Art. 67 TUIR — a strong argument for a buy-and-hold horizon. EU non-residents have full access to *cedolare secca*; income is taxable in Italy under all standard OECD-model treaties with home-country credit relief, eliminating double taxation.

## Tourism, infrastructure and demographic tailwinds

The macro story for Bari is genuinely supportive on a 5–10 year view. **Puglia hit 6.7M arrivals and 22.7M overnights in 2025** (+12.9% / +10.4% YoY); international arrivals grew +25.1%, with France, Germany, the USA, Argentina (+70%), and Brazil (+46%) leading. **Bari province led all Puglia provinces with +16.9% arrivals growth in 2025**. Tourism contributes **13.3% of regional GDP (~€10.4–10.9bn)** and **228,000 jobs (15.4% of regional employment)**.

The infrastructure pipeline is substantial. **Bari Karol Wojtyła airport hit ~8M passengers in 2025** (Top-15 European airport for growth), with United Airlines's New York direct service (4 weekly from May 2026), expanding Ryanair, Wizz Air, EasyJet, and ITA networks, and planned Gulf and Canada intercontinental routes. The **Port of Bari** handled 550,817 cruise passengers in 2025 (+19% YoY) plus 1M ferry passengers to Albania, Greece, Croatia, and Montenegro; MSC's 10-year concession commits to 232 calls by 2030 and 254 by 2034. The **Naples–Bari high-speed rail** — €6bn+ flagship PNRR project — has slipped from 2026 to **late 2027/2028**, but when complete will cut Bari–Naples to 2 hours and Bari–Rome to 3 hours, structurally re-pricing the city's connectivity. Locally, **€159M of PNRR-funded BRT** (Bus Rapid Transit) construction began in October 2025 with completion targeted for 2026/early 2027.

The headwinds are demographic and execution-related. **Bari city population fell 3.6% over the decade to 315,473 in 2025** and could drop below 300,000 by 2040 — although +17% growth in non-EU residents (now 39,090 in metro Bari) partly offsets the trend. Puglia's GDP grew only +0.5% in 2024 and +0.3% in H1 2025, underperforming the national average. PNRR execution lags badly — only 14% of works completed by end-2024. These are reasons to underwrite the investment on **cash yield, not capital appreciation**.

## How Bari compares to alternatives

For a €100K budget, only a handful of Puglia and southern Italian markets are genuinely viable, and each has trade-offs:

**Lecce** is the strongest direct alternative — at €1,498–1,915/sqm it is roughly **30% cheaper than Bari**, €100K buys a 50–60 sqm turnkey apartment in the centro storico, and the city offers a similar dual demand engine (Università del Salento with ~22,000 students plus baroque-tourism inflows). Yields run 5–7% long-term and 8–10% summer STR. Lecce trades depth of market (smaller airport, weaker year-round flows) for affordability and lower seismic and crime risk.

**Polignano a Mare** (€2,696–3,144/sqm) and **Monopoli centro storico** (€2,500–3,250/sqm) are *not realistic* at €100K for a viable STR product — the budget buys only 30–40 sqm, often to renovate, in a market where Monopoli rents have already fallen **-11.6% YoY (August 2025)** and Ostuni Costa rents **-23% YoY** by March 2026. These are cooling signals at the top of the Adriatic premium coast.

**Brindisi** (€1,200–1,400/sqm) puts a 70–90 sqm turnkey within reach but offers thinner tourist demand and weaker resale liquidity. Outside Puglia, **Catania (€1,148/sqm) and Palermo (€1,295–1,691/sqm)** offer the cheapest entries into a major rental market with 7%+ yields, and **Genova at €2,594/sqm produces Italy's highest big-city gross yield at ~7%** — but each carries idiosyncratic risks (Sicily building irregularities, Genova humidity and demographics). **Naples** at €2,978/sqm city average is Italy's #1 investment market (37.3% of purchases for investment per Tecnocasa) but pushes a €100K budget to peripheral or 30–40 sqm central units.

**Net of all this, Bari remains the highest-quality risk-adjusted choice for this profile** — it combines tourism upside with the unique student-demand cushion that pure-tourist towns lack, and the +20% Puglia price appreciation since 2021 has not yet pushed Bari city out of reach the way it has Polignano or Ostuni.

## Risks the consultant must flag

Six risks deserve explicit underwriting:

**Regulatory tightening on STR is the highest-probability risk.** The 2026 budget law dropped the entrepreneurial threshold from 5 to 3 properties; Florence has banned new STR in its UNESCO centre (May 2025); the Constitutional Court's sentence 186/2025 upheld Tuscany's regional law allowing municipal STR caps; Emilia-Romagna is preparing similar legislation; Bari has not yet acted but the municipal *imposta di soggiorno* take rose from €3.6M (2024) to a projected €4M+ (2025), drawing political attention.

**Building abuse (*abuso edilizio*) is the most expensive transaction risk.** ISTAT estimates ~20% of Italian properties are completely illegal builds; Assovib estimates ~40% have compliance issues blocking sale. Puglia and Salento are flagged by Legambiente *Mare Monstrum* as highest-risk regions for coastal *abusivismo*. Buyers inherit criminal liability under Art. 44 DPR 380/2001 — fines up to €103,290, demolition orders, possible confiscation. **Independent geometra and lawyer due diligence (€1,500–€3,000) is non-negotiable**; never use the seller's professionals.

**Tenant protection** makes long-term *libero* contracts unsuitable for absentee landlords — eviction takes 6–24 months. The mitigation is strategic, not tactical: avoid 4+4 contracts entirely, use *transitorio* or *canone concordato studente*, demand 3-month deposits plus *fideiussione* guarantor, and verify lease registration with Agenzia delle Entrate.

**STR market saturation is real but not yet acute in Bari.** Listings grew +250% from 2017–2024, occupancy sits at 44% median, and the "Pentiti di Airbnb" phenomenon emerged in Rome post-Giubileo 2025. Stress-test underwriting at 50–55% occupancy and 26% future tax.

**Property condition and EU energy efficiency rules** require attention. Over 50% of Italian residential stock was Class F or G until 2023; the **EU EPBD Directive 2024/1275** (Italian implementation by May 2026) requires Class E by 2030 and Class D by 2033, with 25% of worst-performing buildings upgraded by 2033. Adriatic salt-air humidity and uninsulated masonry are common in Bari's older stock.

**Crime mapping matters at the neighborhood level.** Bari has 11 confirmed mafia clans (DIA report); the **Strisciuglio clan dominates parts of Libertà, San Paolo, Enziteto, Carbonara, and outer Borgo Antico**; Parisi-Palermiti controls parts of Japigia. Avoid deep San Paolo, Libertà periphery, and Enziteto for any purchase. Numbeo's 2025 crime index for Bari is 50.47 (medium-high).

## Final recommendation and action plan

**The verdict: invest, with the following structure.** Buy a **70–90 sqm 3-bedroom apartment in San Pasquale Bassa, Carrassi Basso, or Picone** at €1,300–€1,600/sqm, in *buono usato* condition, ideally within 1 km of the Politecnico/UniBA Campus on Via Amendola or the Policlinico. Budget €85,000–€90,000 for the property itself, €5,000–€10,000 for furnishing three single rooms, and €13,000–€15,000 for transaction costs — fitting cleanly within €100K total. Lease under **canone concordato studente** at the 10% *cedolare secca*, targeting **€11,000–€12,000 gross annual revenue and ~€8,500 net = approximately 8.5% net yield**. This profile maximizes risk-adjusted return, minimizes regulatory exposure, eliminates eviction risk via the short-contract structure, and rides Bari's structural student-housing shortage.

**The action plan in sequence**: (1) obtain a **codice fiscale** at the nearest Italian consulate or by power of attorney to a Bari lawyer (free, 1–4 weeks); (2) open a **conto corrente non-residenti** with Fineco or Intesa for IMU, condo, and rental flows; (3) engage an **independent bilingual *avvocato* (€1,500–€3,000), *geometra* (€300–€800), and *commercialista* (€500–€1,500/year)** — never the seller's professionals; (4) screen listings on Immobiliare.it and Idealista in San Pasquale, Carrassi, and Picone, prioritizing post-2000 builds with elevators and APE Class E or better; (5) verify *conformità catastale*, *conformità urbanistica*, *agibilità*, condominium *regolamento* (ensure rentals to students are permitted), 5 years of *verbali assemblea*, and absence of demolition orders before *compromesso*; (6) negotiate a *caparra confirmatoria* of 10–15% rather than 20–30%; (7) at *rogito*, elect 26% *imposta sostitutiva* on any future capital gain; (8) immediately register for **CIS Puglia** in the regional DMS, file SCIA/CIA, install required safety equipment, and contract a local property manager for tenant placement.

**The red flags that should kill any specific deal**: cadastral/planning non-conformity that the seller refuses to remediate at their cost; APE rating G with no renovation budget; condominium that prohibits short or per-room leases; pending extraordinary works (lift, façade, roof) with assessments not yet allocated; properties in Strisciuglio-controlled San Paolo, Enziteto, or deep Libertà periphery; and any "off-the-books" price negotiation suggesting under-declaration in the deed (a legacy Italian practice that creates downstream tax and resale problems).

**Conclusion.** Bari in 2026 sits in a rare window where infrastructure, tourism, and demographic-by-immigration tailwinds compound a structural student-rental shortage — and where €100,000 is exactly enough to capture a working investment unit, but only if the investor accepts that *the prime tourist zones are out of budget* and pivots to the student-rental thesis instead. Done correctly, the investment delivers ~8.5% net yield in a stable EU jurisdiction with full *cedolare secca* access, no FX risk, and a 5-year capital gains exemption. Done wrong — chasing Bari Vecchia or Murat with insufficient budget, locking into a 4+4 *libero* contract, or skipping due diligence — it becomes a textbook southern-Italian property trap. The strategy makes the difference, more than the city does.
